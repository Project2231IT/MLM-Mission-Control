# 5 Questions for Ella & Ashton
_Context: We're classifying every booth by category using sales data. Before we finalize the taxonomy and use it for recruitment decisions, we need your domain knowledge on these specific items._

---

**1. Multi-category booths — how do you think about them?**

Many booths sell across multiple categories. For example, Booth 810 (Bethany Johnson) sells both jewelry and women's clothing. Booth 619 (Brad Greene) sells cowhides, vintage furniture, AND clothing. Booth 510 (Brenda Mansell) sells candles, socks, dip mixes, and beauty products.

**Question:** When you think about what a booth "is" — do you think about it by what brings people TO the booth (their draw), or by what makes up most of their sales revenue? Is there a natural way you already mentally categorize these combo booths?

---

**2. What categories should Miss Lucille's NOT add more of?**

Grant flagged two guardrails: no more food vendors (competes with the cafe) and be careful about adding more furniture (don't want to become a furniture dealer). 

**Question:** Are there any other categories you'd put on a "do not recruit" or "enough already" list? For example: do we have too many vintage/antique furniture booths? Too many clothing vendors? Is there anything that doesn't fit the marketplace identity you're building?

---

**3. Which booths punch above their weight — and why?**

The sales data shows who sells the most. But you know things the data doesn't — like which booths customers specifically ask about, which vendors drive foot traffic to their whole aisle, or which booths have a loyal following that comes in just for them.

**Question:** Name 3-5 booths that you consider the "anchors" of the marketplace — the ones that if they left, you'd feel the impact beyond just their sales numbers. What makes them special?

---

**4. What's the difference between a "good fit" and a "bad fit" vendor?**

When you're reviewing applications and looking for new vendors, you mentioned sometimes there aren't strong candidates or they're not right for a particular location.

**Question:** What specifically makes you say "this one isn't right"? Is it the product type, the person, the presentation quality, the effort level? If you could describe your ideal new vendor in 2-3 sentences, what would that look like?

---

**5. Are there product categories customers ask for that you DON'T currently have?**

You hear what customers want every day. You know what they come looking for and leave without buying.

**Question:** What do customers ask for or look for that the marketplace currently doesn't offer or doesn't have enough of? This is the single most valuable recruiting signal we can get — demand that has no supply.

---

_Please answer as specifically as you can. Names, booth numbers, and examples are more useful than general statements. There are no wrong answers — we're building the recruiting playbook from your knowledge combined with the sales data._
